Data and results

Every computational output from the coordination model, with the code that produces each one. The theory and the formalism live on the Methodology tab; this page collects the numbers and the figures. All of it runs on numpy and matplotlib, and the code and the data fetch live in the repository under model/.

1. Fit to CMU motion-capture

We fit the Gaussian graphical model to CMU Graphics Lab motion-capture for three tasks, walk, run, and jump, with one subject and two trials each, over 50 rotational degrees of freedom. The fit tests the model’s three premises, that movement uses few dimensions, that its dependence structure follows anatomy, and that the sign of coordination changes from one movement to another. The code is model/fit_ggm.py, and model/fetch_cmu.sh downloads the six trials first.

(A) Effective dimensionality

The raw-covariance participation ratio is dominated by a few large-amplitude joints and overstates the collapse; the standardized (correlation) ratio is the honest measure. Even standardized, about five of the fifty dimensions carry the movement.

(D) The measured relative phase

Section (C) shows the sign of coordination flips across tasks. Here we recover the relative phase itself, the timing offset between two paired limbs, from the joint-angle time series with a Hilbert transform, so that reading a coupling sign as a relative-phase order parameter rests on a measured phase and not on a sign alone. Walking and running lock the paired limbs near 180 degrees, the anti-phase attractor, and jumping near 0 degrees, the in-phase attractor, with phase-locking values from 0.90 to 0.99, where 1 marks perfect locking. Relative phase is well defined for the rhythmic tasks. The jump column reads as in-phase timing rather than a limit-cycle phase.

2. Fit to real Taijiquan motion (UMONS-TAICHI)

Section 1 fits the model to walking, running, and jumping, which test the machinery but sit far from the movement this project studies. This section runs the same fit on the movement itself, and it does so on two recordings of that movement at once. UMONS-TAICHI captured every session with two instruments running together, a Kinect V2, which infers twenty-five body joints from a depth camera with no markers at thirty frames a second, and a Qualisys optical system, which tracks sixty-eight physical markers on named anatomical landmarks at a hundred and seventy-nine frames a second and whose gaps were filled and cleaned by hand. The Kinect is cheap and quick and its joints are a model’s best guess. The Qualisys is the research standard and its joints sit on real bone. Having both lets us do the one thing a single recording cannot, separate a property of the movement from a property of the instrument, by asking whether a result survives when the body is measured a better way. Twelve people performed thirteen Taijiquan techniques, and three judges scored each performer on a nought-to-ten scale, so the recording carries a skill gradient from expert (mean 8.7 to 9.6) down to novice (mean 5.0 to 6.1). From each recording we read the same ten joint angles, the flexion at the two elbows, two shoulders, two hips, two knees, the spine, and the neck.

Data source: UMONS-TAICHI, Numediart Institute, University of Mons, Belgium, by M. Tits, S. Laraba, E. Caulier, J. Tilmanne and T. Dutoit, published in Data in Brief (2018), doi:10.1016/j.dib.2018.05.088. Archived at zenodo.org/records/2784581 under a Creative Commons BY-NC-SA 4.0 licence. The Kinect skeletons drive model/fit_umons.py; the Qualisys markers drive model/fit_qualisys.py.

(A) The collapse holds, and it holds on both instruments

Data used, both instruments. The participation ratio, defined in section 1, counts the effective dimensions a movement uses. We measured it on the Kinect skeletons (1,793 clips of at least sixty frames) and again on the Qualisys markers (2,147 clips), building the ten angles from the Kinect’s inferred joints in the first case and from marker triads on real landmarks in the second, a knee from the two markers on the knee, a wrist from the two on the wrist. Why both. The collapse is the model’s founding assumption, so before anything rests on it we want it confirmed by two instruments that fail in different ways, not by one. Result. Within a single gesture the ten angles collapse onto about four effective axes on the Kinect (median 3.85) and about three on the Qualisys markers (median near 2.7), in every one of the thirteen techniques, and the count does not move with skill. Two instruments that estimate the body by unrelated means agree that real Taijiquan runs on a handful of dimensions, so the collapse is a property of the movement.

(B) The coordination graph recovers the body’s own tree

Data used, Qualisys markers. The partial correlations, the edge weights of the graphical model from section 1(B), read out which joint angles move together once every other angle is held fixed, so that only the direct link between a given two remains. What matters is the strength, the distance from zero, not the sign, which records only whether two angles open together or one opens as the other closes. Why the markers. This is a fine claim about which particular joints couple, so it needs joints that sit on real bone. The Kinect’s joints are a model’s inference, and on them the graph was too noisy to trust, recovering the anatomical tree only at chance. On the Qualisys markers, pooled over the three experts, the graph is clean. Its strongest edges run down the legs, the hip to the knee within each limb, and it recovers the body’s anatomical tree at an AUC of 0.72 to 0.78, the same range the CMU fit reached and well above the chance value of 0.5, where 1 is perfect. On top of the tree it holds one clear off-anatomy coupling, a cross-body diagonal that ties each elbow to the opposite shoulder, the upper-body cross-connection Taijiquan instruction names directly. Why it matters. A graph that recovers real anatomy on the target movement is a faithful readout, so what it reports about skill in part (C) can be trusted rather than read as noise.

(C) What tracks skill is the timing of the coordination, not a static amount of it

This is where the two instruments part, and the parting is the finding. A first pass on the Kinect misled us. On the thirty-frame Kinect a static measure, the strength of the lower-body coupling, rose with judged skill at a rank correlation of 0.71, which looked like a clean result. It does not survive. On the Qualisys markers the same measure reverses, to a rank correlation of −0.51, and performer by performer the two instruments disagree about who has the more tightly coupled legs, so their leg-coupling scores are themselves negatively related. A number that changes sign when the body is measured better is a property of the Kinect’s joint inference, not of the movement, and we set it aside. Catching it is the whole reason for carrying two instruments.

Data used, Qualisys markers at 179 Hz. The theory on the Methodology tab, after Haken, Kelso and Bunz, does not predict that skill is a larger static amount of coupling. It predicts that skill is a steadier relative phase, a coordination held to the same timing offset cycle after cycle, the order parameter kept stable. Testing timing needs frames fast enough to resolve it, which the thirty-frame Kinect does not have and the hundred-and-seventy-nine-frame Qualisys does, so this measure runs on the markers alone. For each performer we measured the phase-locking value between the two arms, how steadily the left and right arm hold a fixed relative phase across a gesture, where 1 is a phase locked rigidly and 0 is a phase that drifts. Result. It rises with judged skill at a rank correlation of 0.68, holds at a p-value of 0.015, and stays positive when any single performer is dropped. The experts hold the steadier arm coordination; the novices let it drift. Why it matters. Skill sits exactly where the theory places the target of a teaching image, in the stability of the coordinative timing rather than in a static structural quantity, and it took the higher-resolution instrument to see it.

The low-dimensional coordination is what the practice already shares, expert and novice alike. What skill adds is not a larger amount of coupling, which the better instrument shows does not track skill at all, but a steadier hold on the timing of the coordination the movement already has. A teaching image and a two-word correction are aimed at exactly this, at how a coordination is timed rather than at how much of it there is. Whether that steadiness rises in a single learner as a teacher works on them, rather than differing between the separate trained and untrained people captured here, is the question a longitudinal recording would answer, and it is where a controlled experiment would make its case.

Reproduce: cd model && bash fetch_umons.sh && python3 fit_umons.py (Kinect) and bash fetch_qualisys.sh && python3 fit_qualisys.py (Qualisys).

3. Two-body convergence (step 1)

Step 1 of the intended outcome asks whether a metaphor drives two differently built bodies onto the same relative-phase coordination, where a joint-angle instruction cannot. We ran it on the same model, with no new data. A teacher trained to an anti-phase (mirror) arm coordination sits at a relative-phase order parameter r = −0.69. Two learners with different anatomy, a different baseline precision and different segment lengths, start in-phase, on the opposite side of zero. As the shared metaphor strengthens, both learners cross zero and settle near the teacher, so the gap closes from about 1.3 to about 0.1. A joint-angle instruction sets a mean pose and never touches the precision matrix, so its gap stays flat at every strength.

4. Frustration, conflicting metaphors freeze the body (step 3)

Step 3 tests the prediction from the Methodology page that two conflicting teaching images cannot both be satisfied, so the coordination they share freezes. We load an in-phase coupling at weight W(1−λ) and an anti-phase coupling at weight Wλ on the same arm pair, and sweep the conflict λ from one image to the other. At λ = ½ the two demands are equal. The off-diagonal coupling passes through zero while both collective modes stiffen, so the cheapest coordinated mode’s variance drops about sevenfold, from 1.88 to 0.28, and the arm correlation passes through 0.00. Two images cancel rather than average into a compromise. No new data, the sweep runs on the model.

5. Code and reproducibility

The repository holds everything under model/.

  • taichi_model.py, the core Gaussian graphical model of body coordination.
  • fit_ggm.py, the fit to CMU motion-capture, with fetch_cmu.sh to download the trials.
  • relative_phase.py, the measured relative phase of section 1(D), needs scipy.
  • fit_umons.py, the fit to real Taijiquan motion, with fetch_umons.sh to download the Kinect skeletons.
  • two_body.py, the two-body convergence of step 1.
  • frustration.py, the frustration sweep of step 3.
  • README.md, the run instructions and the committed results.
cd model
bash fetch_cmu.sh && python3 fit_ggm.py   # section 1 A-C
python3 relative_phase.py                 # section 1 D
bash fetch_umons.sh && python3 fit_umons.py  # section 2
python3 two_body.py                       # section 3
python3 frustration.py                    # section 4
Provenance. Section 1 fits the model to the CMU Graphics Lab Motion Capture Database (mocap.cs.cmu.edu), subjects walking, running, and jumping. Section 2 fits it to the UMONS-TAICHI database (zenodo.org/records/2784581, CC BY-NC-SA 4.0), twelve people performing thirteen Taijiquan techniques. Sections 3 and 4 run on the same model with no new data. The theory behind all of it, and the full citation list, appear on the Methodology tab.
