Metaphor × Tai-Chi — Bridge Dossier

Built from the curated cognitive-science corpus (Supabase journal_articles): the K=16 Foster DATM over the curated 268, concept probes over 2,743 embedded docs (text-embedding-3-small), and an embedding-space gap analysis. Generated 2026-07-03.

The corpus was assembled to locate one thing, the point where analogical structure meets embodied skill. This dossier reports where the corpus already makes that crossing, who to cite together, what the concept probes surfaced beyond the hand-built list, and where the gap that the article can fill actually sits.

1. The seam, stated

Analogical reasoning is standardly modelled as structure-mapping, the alignment of the relational structure of a source domain onto a target (Gentner). Skilled bodily movement, in the tai-chi tradition and in the motor-learning literature, is standardly modelled as implicit, perception-action coupling that resists explicit rule-statement (Turvey, Wulf). The corpus shows these two pictures are not rivals and not strangers. They meet wherever a mover acquires a movement by an analogy, holds a posture as a structured image, or lets a felt bodily dynamic carry a cross-domain mapping. The candidate claim: the tai-chi form is a metaphor executed in the body, a structure-mapping whose target domain is the mover's own sensorimotor field, and its intelligibility is the same relational alignment that analogy theory describes, run in a register where the aligned structure is felt rather than judged.

4. What the probes surfaced beyond the curated list

Seven concept probes (cosine over the full 2,743-doc corpus) pulled seam material the hand-built list missed. Highest-value new finds, with similarity:

  • Gallagher, Enactive Metaphors: Learning Through Full-Body Engagement (0.62 on enactive-synergies) — arguably the single most on-point paper for the thesis, and not previously in the list.
  • Hipólito, Embodied skillful performance: where the action is (0.54) — skill without representation, directly usable.
  • Hostetter & Alibali, Visible embodiment: Gestures as simulated action (0.58) — gesture as simulated action, the mechanism under Mittelberg.
  • Schack, Mental representation and motor imagery training (0.65) and Bennet, Shared mechanisms underlie mental imagery and motor planning (0.64) — the motor-imagery / cognitive-action-architecture literature, a whole adjacent vein for the "form held as image" claim.
  • Kiverstein, Reconceiving representation-hungry cognition: an ecological-enactive account (0.62) — the theoretical frame for treating relational structure ecologically.
  • Waltz, The role of working memory in analogical mapping (0.57) — the cognitive-load hinge between mapping and skilled execution.
  • Henner, American Sign Language Syntax and Analogical Reasoning Skills — a literal analogy × bodily-language paper the probes flagged as a cross-side connector.

The analogy-for-motor probe is the tightest (Liao & Masters at 0.68, Abrahamson at 0.60), confirming that the "learn the movement by analogy" tradition is the corpus's densest bridge.

5. The gap: adjacency without synthesis

The two literatures are not far apart in embedding space. Within-analogy cosine is 0.43, within-embodiment 0.44, and analogy↔embodiment 0.38, a separation ratio of only 1.13×; 46% of analogy documents have at least one embodiment document among their fifteen nearest neighbors. The raw materials sit side by side.

What is scarce is explicit synthesis. The DATM recovers only a handful of genuine analogy×embodiment atoms, and the subcategories that already fuse the sides (analogy-in-motor-learning, grounded metaphor) hold roughly ten documents out of the curated 268, under 1%. Most tellingly, the tai-chi-as-analogy probe tops out at 0.46, and every hit is clinical or neuroscientific (brain morphometry, mood, aging). The corpus's tai-chi holdings treat the practice as a health intervention, not as a structured image grasped by analogy. That absence is the article's opening: the phenomenological and analogical reading of the form is a move almost no one in the corpus has made.

The gap, precisely: proximity without integration. The fields are adjacent, the connectors exist (Liao & Masters, Abrahamson, Gallagher, Kimmel, Hutchins), but no one has stated the general claim that a structure-mapping and a learned movement are the same operation in two registers. Turvey and Gentner are never in the same paragraph.

8. The contemporary conversation (2021–2026, outside the corpus)

An OpenAlex pull (2021–2026, on-topic, deduplicated against the library) is noisier than the internal probes, but five recent papers land on the seam and are not yet held:

  • Woods, Rudd & Gray (2021), Enskilment: an Ecological-Anthropological Worldview of Skill, Learning and Education in Sport (Sports Medicine Open, 44 cites) — the ecological-skill frame the embodiment column needs, sport-facing.
  • Priorelli & Pezzulo (2023), Deep kinematic inference affords efficient and scalable control of bodily movements (PNAS) — goal-directed movement as mapping from extrinsic to intrinsic to motor coordinates, structure-mapping under a different name, in active-inference terms.
  • Khatin-Zadeh et al. (2022), The roles of motion, gesture, and embodied action in the processing of mathematical concepts (Frontiers in Psychology) — gesture and motion as the vehicle of abstract-concept processing, directly downstream of Abrahamson.
  • Ibbotson (2023), The Development of Executive Function (J. Cognition and Development) — domain-general analogical processes building a functional hierarchy of skills, the developmental route from analogy to skill.
  • Sanches de Oliveira & van Es (2023), Scientific practice as ecological-enactive co-construction (Synthese) — the ecological-enactive framing at the level of practice, useful for the theoretical scaffold.
