Methodology, the coordination model

We build the model in the modelling strand of our project. This page describes the model, where we drew it from, why we gave it this form, how closely its premises match real motion-capture data, and the experiment those results justify.

1. Inception, and why this model

The five of us study how a teacher passes embodied skill to a learner when words cannot capture the skill. Polanyi poses this problem, and we take it into the setting of movement (Polanyi, 1966; Miton & DeDeo, 2022). We argue that a metaphor transmits the skill from the teacher to the learner. A teaching image such as pearls on a string or cloud hands gives the learner a relational structure to enact, and that structure matches the mapping that structure-mapping theory describes (Gentner, 1983). The learner feels the structure rather than reasoning it out. In our corpus work, the topic map and the bridge dossier, we find that this connection already appears across the literature. The corpus does not exhibit the mechanism at work in a body, and it does not specify what it means for a metaphor to constrain movement.

A tai-chi teacher utters a metaphor, an image of the form A is B, rather than the four-term proportion that defines a strict analogy. We therefore call the phenomenon a metaphor, and we borrow the theory of analogy to explain its mechanism. That theory, Gentner’s structure-mapping, describes how the relational structure of a metaphor transfers to its target (Gentner, 1988). The embodied-metaphor tradition studies the same transfer as it happens in the body rather than in conscious thought (Abrahamson, 2020; Kimmel et al., 2024).

In this first attempt, a rough model, we represent a metaphor as a set of couplings on the body’s joint angles. We draw on three traditions. From statistical physics, through Gaussian graphical models and the Ising model, we take the couplings, their signs, and the frustration that arises when constraints conflict. From motor control we take our target, Bernstein’s degrees-of-freedom problem, the question of how a body controls more joints than any single movement needs. From cognitive science we take one commitment, that a metaphor transfers relational structure and not particular values. This first version left gaps, and at the outset we did not know whether to keep the approach.

In this page we test that first attempt. We state its formalism plainly enough to judge, we fit it to real motion-capture data, and we mark where it fails. Setting the rough execution aside, the three premises the model depends on all prove true of real movement (§4). Movement uses few dimensions, coordination follows anatomy, and the sign of a coupling encodes the content a metaphor transmits. That result justifies a serious second version. The limits (§5) and the intended outcome (§6) describe what such a version would require.

We keep one choice from the first attempt, and the rest of the page depends on it. The model gives the modeller two parameters that do not interfere with each other. The coupling structure sets how many dimensions of movement survive, and the temperature sets only how widely those dimensions spread. Because the two stay separate, the model keeps apart two claims that the literature easily runs together. A metaphor constrains coordination. Practice reduces variability.

2. What the model claims, before the algebra

A body has many joints, and therefore many degrees of freedom (DOF). We represent a body as a probability distribution over joint configurations, and the two parameters control that distribution.

  • Coupling structure (the matrix J) sets the effective dimensionality of movement, that is, how many independent directions the body explores. When we represent a metaphor, we add couplings that bind otherwise-independent joints into a synergy and lower the dimension.
  • Temperature (the scalar T) sets only the variability, the overall spread of the movement. It leaves the shape of the coordination unchanged.

The two parameters vary independently, so the model keeps two easily-confused claims apart. Whether a metaphor constrains coordination depends on J alone. Whether practice reduces variability depends on T alone. In Section 3 we make this separation exact and add signs, so that a coupling can carry relational content rather than merely bind. In Section 4 we ask whether real bodies behave the way the model needs.

3. The formalism

State and energy

Let x ∈ ℝn denote the vector of joint-angle deviations from a fixed neutral pose, one number per DOF. A symmetric positive-definite precision matrix J assigns each configuration a cost, a quadratic energy.

E(x) = ½ x⊤J x

Distribution

At temperature T, a Boltzmann distribution over that energy gives a zero-mean Gaussian, whose covariance equals the inverse precision scaled by T.

p(x) ∝ exp(−E(x)/T)  ⇒  x ~ 𝒩(0, Σ),  Σ = T J−1

The covariance Σ encodes all of the movement’s content, that is, how the joints co-vary, since the mean pose stays fixed at neutral. This choice fixes the model’s first real commitment. A joint-angle target specifies a single point, whereas a metaphor specifies a whole shape of covariation, so an image can instruct where a list of angles cannot.

Coupling structure from a graph Laplacian

The precision matrix combines a baseline stiffness with a weighted graph Laplacian of the coupling graph. For an edge set with weights, the Laplacian L = D − W subtracts the adjacency from the degree. Adding γI gives every DOF a finite variance of its own and keeps J positive-definite.

J = γI + Lanatomy + β Lmetaphor  L PSD, γ > 0 ⇒ J ≻ 0

Here Lanatomy denotes the body’s own linkage graph, a weak fixed scaffold; β denotes the strength of the imposed metaphor; Lmetaphor denotes a clique over the joints the metaphor binds. When we add metaphor edges, the bound joints co-vary and the movement collapses onto a lower-dimensional manifold, a synergy.

Effective dimensionality, and why temperature cannot touch it

The participation ratio reads effective DOF off the covariance spectrum {λi}.

PR(Σ) = (∑i λi)2∑i λi2  ∈  [1, n]

PR = n when the variance spreads equally over all directions, which means no coordination. PR approaches 1 when a single collective coordinate dominates, which means one synergy. Because Σ = T J−1 multiplies every eigenvalue by the same T, the ratio does not change, so raising the temperature enlarges the movement without reshaping it. This separation is essential to everything that follows. Dimensionality belongs to J alone, and variability belongs to T alone.

Signs: from binding to relation

A plain Laplacian can only make joints move together, giving a positive correlation. To let a metaphor specify the relation the joints hold, and not just that they coordinate, each coupling term penalizes a signed linear combination.

Eterm = ½ w (a xi − b xj)2

This term adds wa2 to Jii, wb2 to Jjj, and −wab to Jij. The sign of the product ab sets the relation. Every term forms a perfect square, so it stays positive-semidefinite, and J stays positive-definite no matter how many terms we add.

Here the model reaches its second real commitment. The participation ratio does not depend on sign, because an anti-phase synergy collapses exactly as many dimensions as an in-phase one. The pattern of the surviving mode separates the two, in the structure of the correlation signs. The participation ratio therefore measures the magnitude of a metaphor’s constraint, how far it collapses the movement, which answers Bernstein’s question. The signs carry its relational content, which coordination it imposes and where the analogy’s structure is contained. We apply structure-mapping here in a motor register, where the specific angles vary randomly under sampling and do not persist, whereas the relations, the couplings, remain stable across samples.

Frustration: conflicting metaphors freeze the body

Two metaphors that specify incompatible relations on the same joints cannot both hold. Suppose we load an in-phase coupling at weight W(1−λ) and an anti-phase coupling at weight Wλ on the same pair. The off-diagonal becomes the signed sum of the two, and each diagonal gains from both.

Jij = −W(1−λ) + Wλ = −W(1−2λ)  → 0 at λ = ½, while Jii, Jjj each gain W

At λ = ½ the off-diagonal passes through zero while both joints stiffen, so the collective coordinates c± = (xL ± xR)/√2 stiffen too, and no low-energy coordinated motion remains. Conflicting instructions do not average. They cancel, and the coordination collapses through zero. In a Gaussian energy this loss removes a soft mode rather than opening the rugged degeneracy of a real spin glass, but the movement suffers the same way. The body loses its one low-energy coordinated direction. Two incompatible teaching images therefore degrade coordination rather than combining into a compromise.

4. Does a real body behave this way? A proof of concept

The formalism makes three assumptions about real movement. Movement uses few dimensions. Its conditional-dependence structure follows anatomy. The sign of coordination changes from one movement to another even at a similar number of dimensions. We tested each assumption by fitting the Gaussian graphical model to CMU Graphics Lab motion-capture data. For each trial we took the per-frame joint Euler angles, removed global position and orientation, centred and pooled the trials within a task, and estimated the covariance and precision across frames. The data cover three tasks (walk, run, jump), one subject and two trials each, and 50 rotational DOF once we drop near-constant joints.

(A) Movement is low-dimensional

We report two participation ratios, because they answer different questions. A few large-amplitude joints dominate the raw-covariance participation ratio, so it overstates the collapse. The standardized (correlation) participation ratio measures more honestly how many coordinated directions stay active. Even standardized, about five of the fifty dimensions account for the movement. This matches the synergy literature, where a few principal components account for most of the postural variance (Santello et al., 1998; Turvey, 2007).

We build J from a graph Laplacian of the anatomical linkage. If that construction is correct, the estimated partial correlations, the edge weights of the Gaussian graphical model, should peak between anatomically adjacent joints. They do, by a factor of about two. The separation between adjacent and non-adjacent pairs climbs well above chance (AUC 0.5 = chance, 1 = perfect).

(C) Same body, different task, the sign flips

Here the central result appears. Across the three tasks, the correlation between the matched flexion axes of paired limbs changes sign, while the effective dimensionality barely moves. Walking and running couple the limbs anti-phase, since they alternate; jumping couples them in-phase, since they extend together. The collapse stays the same in every case, but the relational content flips. The participation ratio does not depend on sign, as we predicted above, and that independence now appears in real human movement.

The premises prove true. We still cannot claim that a metaphor transmits a skill, because a task only substitutes for an instruction, and a single body plays both teacher and learner. We address exactly that gap in Section 6.

5. Honest limits

  • The task substitutes for the instruction. Walk, run, and jump differ in coordination, but we gave no metaphor. To test the real claim, that a verbal image reshapes J, we need a study that varies the instruction while holding the task fixed.
  • Sign conventions depend on the axis. Only the relative sign structure is meaningful, for example the contrast between contralateral and ipsilateral pairs, or between walk and jump. The absolute sign of any single entry has no meaning on its own.
  • The representations do not match exactly. The model uses one scalar angle per joint, whereas motion capture records three rotational DOF per joint in a real articulated chain. To fit the model we need either a projection of each joint onto its principal rotation axis or a lift of the model to three DOF per joint, a real methods decision rather than a detail.
  • Temperature models expertise only thinly. T scales all directions equally, but an expert’s variance reduction is anisotropic (the uncontrolled-manifold finding; Scholz & Schöner, 1999). An anisotropic reduction itself changes J. So in this model “the learner consolidated the metaphor” and “the learner became expert” can describe one event. We can claim that as a feature on purpose, or we can treat it as a confusion to resolve.
  • The samples are small. The run condition has few frames, so its fit serves only as a proof of concept, rather than as an estimate with error bars.

6. Intended outcome, from working model to fundable experiment

Up to this point we have a coherent mechanism, consistent with real movement. We have not yet shown that a metaphor transmits a skill from one body to another, our actual thesis, and to show it we need a laboratory. The remaining steps fall into two kinds. The computational steps cost only time, and we can carry them out now. One of them turns the model into an experiment worth funding.

  1. Two-body transmission (computational, can be carried out now). We give a teacher and a learner different bodies, that is, a different baseline J and different segment lengths. A joint-angle instruction stays body-specific and fails across the mismatch. A relational coupling, the sign-and-ratio pattern, stays body-invariant and should survive it. If the metaphor rebuilds the teacher’s synergy where a list of joint angles fails, the model stops merely illustrating that a metaphor could transmit tacit skill and begins to show that it does. This step needs no new data. The pitch below depends on its result.
  2. A metaphor-instruction experiment. ◆ DISI PITCH We ask DISI to fund this experiment. We would record full-body kinematics under different metaphorical instructions, with expert and novice or teacher and learner pairs, in a pedagogy that already teaches through metaphor, starting with tai chi and extending to dance, physical therapy, and instrumental posture. We can pre-register the whole study in advance, including the predicted direction of the participation-ratio collapse, the correlation-sign pattern, and the cross-body transfer, together with the analysis. A funded study would then test that prediction rather than search for one. Three conditions already hold. The first condition is that preliminary results exist, on this page and in step 1. The second condition is that precedent exists, since analogy produces implicit motor learning (Liao & Masters, 2001) and since instruction and attention reshape motor performance (Wulf & Lewthwaite, 2016). The third condition is that the method works, since Tits et al. (2018) recorded expert tai-chi motion at exactly this level of detail. The topic fits DISI directly, since embodied skill passes between agents without symbols as a kind of intelligence, and this fit requires no distortion of the project’s own framing.
  3. Frustration as curriculum. We test the prediction that two conflicting teaching images degrade coordination rather than blending. The prediction targets the order and compatibility of instruction, squarely a question about transmission, and it makes a natural second study once we fund the first.
  4. Test the premises on a larger corpus. We refit the dimensionality and anatomy results on a large corpus such as AMASS or the KIT Whole-Body Human Motion Database, using different activities as a first rough proxy for different relational content. The reading list already holds a tai-chi corpus (Tits et al., 2018, expert Taijiquan mocap), so we need not collect the data from scratch.
  5. Make the model and the data use the same representation. We choose either the scalar-axis projection or the three-DOF lift, so that the model and the data use the same geometry and the fit follows a principled method.

We build the model as a Gaussian graphical model over the body’s degrees of freedom, with a graph-Laplacian precision matrix, a signed-coupling extension (in-phase, anti-phase, and fixed-ratio) that we take from the Ising and spin-glass literature, and a temperature that scales variability without changing coordination. The model’s target is Bernstein’s degrees-of-freedom problem, and its commitment to relational content follows Gentner’s structure-mapping. As a proof of concept, we fit the model to CMU Graphics Lab Motion Capture (subjects walking, running, and jumping), using the participation ratio for effective dimensionality, partial correlations for the conditional-dependence graph, and matched-axis correlations for the sign of limb coordination. The repository holds the code and data fetch under model/.
