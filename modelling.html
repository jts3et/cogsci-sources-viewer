Methodology, the coordination model

This page introduces the coordination model, one strand of our project. Five of us at the Diverse Intelligences Summer Institute study how a teacher hands an embodied skill to a learner through a spoken image, and this page sets out the mathematical model we built for that handing. We describe the model, where we drew it from, why we read it as a claim about coordination dynamics rather than transferred structure, how closely its premises match real motion-capture data, and the experiment those results justify. A reader needs no prior training in motor control, physics, or statistics, since we define each technical term in plain words the first time it appears.

1. Inception, and why this model

Begin with the everyday puzzle the whole project circles. A tai-chi teacher tells a beginner to imagine the arm as pearls on a string, and the beginner moves better, though neither one can say in plain instructions what the image corrected. Skills of this kind live below words. A person who balances on a bicycle cannot write down the adjustments that keep the bicycle upright, and an expert who performs a movement smoothly often cannot state the rule the performance follows. Michael Polanyi named this kind of knowing, the knowing that exceeds what a person can put into words, and Hélène Miton and Simon DeDeo model how such knowledge passes from one practitioner to another across generations even though no one involved can fully articulate it (Polanyi, 1966; Miton & DeDeo, 2022). The question we take up narrows that large problem to a single channel. How does a short spoken image change what a learner’s body does.

One tempting picture treats the image as a container that carries a hidden lesson from the teacher into the learner. A child raises a banana to her ear as a telephone. Shaun Gallagher and Robb Lindgren, in “Enactive Metaphors: Learning Through Full-Body Engagement” (2015), find a perception at work in that pretense, the perception a cognitivist would redescribe as a representation, that is, as an internal symbol standing in for something in the world. She sees in the banana a possibility she can act on, on their account “seeing objects as if they were involved in such actions” (Gallagher and Lindgren, 2015, 397). Their as if looks at first like the cognitivist’s opening. Once a learner sees a teacher’s movement as if the skill lived inside it, something stored in the teacher would pass to the learner, the passage Miton and DeDeo, after Polanyi, model as the transmission of tacit knowledge. Gallagher and Lindgren shut that opening as they widen it. The child’s metaphor lives in her hand and in the movement she makes, so the as if stays with her own perceiving of a possible action, and a representation reaching her from outside plays no part. Nothing tacit crosses from one head to another, since no stored structure waits in either to cross.

The body the teacher addresses carries a problem that any account of skill has to face. A human body has far more movable parts than any one movement needs. Each joint can bend in more than one way, and the many joints together can reach a given goal through countless different combinations of angles. The Soviet physiologist Nikolai Bernstein called this surplus the degrees-of-freedom problem, where a degree of freedom is one independent way the body can move, one dial the nervous system could in principle set on its own (Bernstein, 1967). The problem is not how the body finds enough options but how it manages the flood of them, how it controls so many dials at once when a smooth action requires them to change together rather than each on its own. Michael Turvey states the task as the problem of how to “compress the movement system’s state space of very many dimensions” into “a control space of very few dimensions” (Turvey, 2007, 658). A state space here means the full set of all the postures the body could take, one axis for every dial. To compress it into a few dimensions means to tie the dials together so that setting one nearly sets the others, and the body then steers a handful of combined controls instead of dozens of separate ones.

Motor scientists call such a tied-together group of joints a synergy. A synergy is a set of body parts yoked so that they act as a single unit, the many muscles or joints constrained to vary together in a fixed pattern rather than independently. Mark Latash treats the synergy as the nervous system’s working answer to Bernstein’s problem, a way of reducing the number of things that must be controlled by making groups of them move as one (Latash, 2008). The same reduction shows up when researchers record real behavior and count how many independent patterns it takes to reconstruct it. Andrea d’Avella and Emilio Bizzi found that a small number of fixed muscle groupings, combined in different strengths, account for a wide range of natural movements, so that the nervous system appears to build many actions from a few reusable building blocks (d’Avella and Bizzi, 2005). Marco Santello, Martha Flanders, and John Soechting found the same economy in the hand, where a few coordinated patterns of finger motion account for most of the variety in how people grasp and use objects (Santello et al., 1998). A skilled movement, on this reading, is a movement that has found the right few synergies for its task.

Our earlier account of the teaching image borrowed a theory from the study of analogy and read the image as a package of relations shipped from teacher to student. Dedre Gentner’s structure-mapping theory treats an analogy as a relational structure abstracted from one domain, the source, and mapped onto another, the target, so that the pattern of relations holding among the source’s parts is carried over to the target’s parts (Gentner, 1983; Gentner, 1988). Read through that theory, pearls on a string would carry a structure from the image of a beaded string onto the learner’s arm, and learning would consist in the learner copying that structure. Read that way, the account mistakes what happens between two moving bodies. A relational structure abstracted from one body and mapped onto another is a representation the second body would have to copy, and a novice who has never performed the movement holds nothing for the mapping to fasten onto. The structure the theory would transfer describes an expert coordination the beginner does not yet have, so there is no matching structure in the learner for the teacher’s to map onto.

The enactive and embodied traditions give us the register we need instead. Enactive accounts of the mind deny that a learner copies an inner structure at all, and treat a skill as the ongoing coordination of a body with its task, a way of engaging the world rather than a description of it held in the head. Hanne De Jaegher and Ezequiel Di Paolo cast teaching itself as two agents making sense together, each continuously adjusting to the other rather than one uploading a file to the other (De Jaegher & Di Paolo, 2007). Anthony Wilson and Sabrina Golonka press the point that embodied cognition, taken seriously, relocates much of the work of a skill out of internal representations and into the dynamics of the body engaging its environment (Wilson & Golonka, 2013). Jan Degenaar and Kevin O’Regan locate perception and skill in a person’s mastery of the lawful ways sensation changes as the body moves, so that what a learner acquires is an attunement to those regularities rather than a stored picture of them (Degenaar & O’Regan, 2015). On all of these accounts a teaching image does not deposit content. It changes how a moving body organizes itself.

Embodied-metaphor research studies exactly that change, the metaphor as it works in the body rather than in a proposition. George Lakoff and Mark Johnson opened this line by arguing that everyday abstract thought rests on metaphors drawn from bodily experience, so that a figure like rising spirits recruits the felt sense of vertical motion (Lakoff & Johnson, 2008). Dor Abrahamson treats a metaphor as sensorimotor dynamics, a way of setting the body in motion, and Michael Kimmel and colleagues model how an imagined image and the moving body call each other into being in practices such as martial arts and dance (Abrahamson, 2020; Kimmel et al., 2024). Tai chi is a natural test case, since its pedagogy already teaches through images of this kind. Kamila Osypiuk, Evan Thompson, and Peter Wayne read the art through embodied cognitive science, describing how a physical posture and a mental image shape each other in practice (Osypiuk et al., 2018), and Peter Wayne and Mark Fuerst document how much of tai-chi instruction proceeds through exactly such imagery rather than through explicit rules (Wayne & Fuerst, 2013). Marcel Mauss had already observed, long before, that skilled bodily techniques are handed down culturally and vary from society to society, so that even walking and swimming are learned forms rather than natural givens (Mauss, 1973).

The alternative we develop reads the teaching image as a setting on the body’s coordination rather than a structure shipped into it. The relevant physics comes from the study of how systems with many parts settle, on their own, into a few organized patterns. Hermann Haken, J. A. Scott Kelso, and Herbert Bunz built a now-classic model of exactly this in human movement, showing that when a person waves two fingers, the pair spontaneously settles into one of two stable patterns and can switch between them, with the whole coordination summarized by a single quantity (Haken, Kelso & Bunz, 1985; Kelso, 1995). That single summarizing quantity is what physicists call an order parameter, a low-dimensional variable that captures the collective pattern a many-part system has fallen into, the way a single number for average alignment captures whether a piece of iron is magnetized. Gregor Schöner and Kelso showed that a wide range of coordinated movements can be described this way, as the settling of an order parameter into a stable value (Schöner & Kelso, 1988). For the finger model the order parameter is the relative phase, the difference in timing between the two fingers, which we explain in full in section 3. On this reading a metaphor does not transfer a structure. It shifts which stable coordination the body falls into, and two differently built bodies can fall into the same coordination because the same constraint shapes each one, each reaching that coordination through its own joints and its own segment lengths.

In this first attempt, a deliberately rough model, we represent a metaphor as a set of couplings on the body’s joint angles, where a coupling is a mathematical tie that makes two joints tend to move in a fixed relation. We draw on three traditions at once. From statistical physics, through the Ising model and its descendants, we take the couplings, the idea that a coupling carries a sign, and the phenomenon of frustration that arises when two couplings demand incompatible things of the same parts. From motor control we take the target, Bernstein’s degrees-of-freedom problem, the question of how a body governs more joints than any single movement needs. From the enactive and embodied strands of cognitive science we take one commitment, that a metaphor fixes a relation between joints and leaves the particular joint values free to vary. This first version left gaps, and at the outset we did not know whether to keep the approach.

In this page we test that first attempt. We state its formalism plainly enough to judge, we fit it to real motion-capture data, and we mark where it fails. Setting the rough execution aside, the three premises the model depends on all prove true of real movement (§4). Movement uses few dimensions, coordination follows anatomy, and the sign of a coupling fixes the relative phase the coordination holds. That result justifies a serious second version. The limits (§5) and the intended outcome (§6) describe what such a version would require.

We keep one choice from the first attempt, and the rest of the page depends on it. The model gives the modeller two separate controls that do not interfere with each other. The coupling structure sets how many dimensions of movement survive, and the temperature sets only how widely those dimensions spread. Because the two stay separate, the model keeps apart two claims that the literature easily runs together. A metaphor constrains coordination. Practice reduces variability. The next section states these two controls in plain terms before section 3 gives the algebra.

2. What the model claims, before the algebra

A body has many joints, and therefore many degrees of freedom (DOF), where a degree of freedom, again, is one independent way the body can move. We represent a body not as a single posture but as a probability distribution over joint configurations, that is, as the whole spread of postures the body visits as it moves, together with how often it visits each. Two numbers-worth of structure control that spread, and each answers a different question about the movement.

  • Coupling structure (the matrix J) sets the effective dimensionality of movement, that is, how many independent directions the body explores. Effective dimensionality counts the genuinely separate patterns in the movement, so a body whose joints all move together has a low effective dimensionality even if it has many joints. When we represent a metaphor, we add couplings that bind otherwise-independent joints into a synergy and lower the dimension.
  • Temperature (the scalar T) sets only the variability, the overall spread of the movement, borrowing the word from physics where temperature measures how much a system jitters. A higher temperature makes the movement larger and looser without changing its shape. It leaves the pattern of the coordination unchanged.

The two controls vary independently, and that independence does real work. Whether a metaphor constrains coordination depends on J alone. Whether practice reduces variability depends on T alone. Keeping them separate lets us say that a beginner and an expert can share the same coordination while differing in how tightly they hold it, or share a looseness while differing in coordination, without the model confusing the two. Section 3 makes this separation exact and adds signs to the couplings, so that a coupling binds two joints and, in the sign it takes, fixes the relation between them, whether they move together or in opposition. In section 4 we ask whether real bodies behave the way the model needs.

Two symbols carry two meanings. λ denotes the covariance eigenvalues above and the conflict parameter in the frustration section, and W denotes the adjacency matrix in the Laplacian and a scalar coupling weight in the frustration section.

State and energy

We describe a posture by how far each joint bends away from a fixed resting pose. Let x ∈ ℝn denote the vector of joint-angle deviations from a fixed neutral pose, one number per DOF, so that x lists, joint by joint, how far the body has departed from rest. We then assign every posture a cost, called an energy, that measures how strained or awkward the posture is, with the resting pose costing nothing and more contorted postures costing more. A symmetric positive-definite precision matrix J sets those costs. Positive-definite means the energy is lowest at rest and rises in every direction away from it, so the resting pose sits at the bottom of a bowl, and the matrix J fixes how steeply the bowl rises along each combination of joints.

E(x) = ½ x⊤J x

The off-diagonal entries of J carry the couplings. When Jij is nonzero, the cost of bending joint i depends on what joint j is doing, so the two joints are no longer priced separately and the cheapest postures are the ones in which they hold a particular relation. The couplings are what turn a bag of independent joints into a coordinated body.

Distribution

A body does not hold one posture. It moves, and it visits some postures more than others. We model that spread with a standard rule from physics, the Boltzmann distribution, which says that low-cost states occur more often than high-cost ones, and that raising the temperature evens out the difference so that costlier states become more common. Applying that rule to the quadratic energy above yields a Gaussian distribution, the familiar bell-shaped spread, centred on the resting pose.

p(x) ∝ exp(−E(x)/T)  ⇒  x ~ 𝒩(0, Σ),  Σ = T J−1

The spread of that Gaussian is summarized by its covariance Σ, a table that records, for every pair of joints, how much they move together. A covariance entry is positive when two joints tend to depart from rest in the same direction, negative when one rises as the other falls, and near zero when they move independently. The covariance equals the inverse of the precision matrix scaled by the temperature. Because we hold the resting pose fixed at the centre, the covariance Σ encodes all of the movement’s content, that is, how the joints co-vary. This is a model built as a Gaussian graphical model, a standard tool in statistics for representing which variables depend on which once the others are accounted for, where the precision matrix J records the direct dependencies and its zeros mark pairs with no direct tie (Lauritzen, 1996). This choice fixes the model’s first real commitment. A joint-angle target, a specific list of angles the learner is told to hit, picks out a single point in configuration space, and a body that differs in its segment lengths reaches that point through a different posture, so a list of target angles fails to transfer across bodies. By constraining the covariation instead, a metaphor fixes a pattern of how joints move together that two differently built bodies can each realize in their own coordinates. An image therefore reshapes coordination where a list of angles cannot.

Coupling structure from a graph Laplacian

We build the precision matrix from a picture of which joints are tied to which, drawn as a graph, that is, as dots for joints and lines for the ties between them. The standard way to turn such a graph into a matrix is the graph Laplacian, written L = D − W, where W records which joints are linked and how strongly (the adjacency), and D records each joint’s total linkage (the degree). Subtracting one from the other produces a matrix whose energy penalizes differences between linked joints, so linked joints prefer to move together. In the model’s code the body’s anatomical links are laid down first as a kinematic tree, the chain of connections from torso to shoulder to elbow to wrist and the matching chain down each leg, given a weak uniform weight. Adding γI, a small stiffness on every joint on its own, gives each DOF a finite variance of its own and keeps J positive-definite, so the energy bowl still has a bottom.

J = γI + Lanatomy + β Lmetaphor  L PSD, γ > 0 ⇒ J ≻ 0

Here Lanatomy denotes the body’s own linkage graph, a weak fixed scaffold present in every posture; β denotes the strength of the imposed metaphor, the dial that says how forcefully the image is applied; and Lmetaphor denotes a clique over the joints the metaphor binds, a clique being a group in which every member is linked to every other. The code makes this concrete. The image pearls on a string binds the shoulder, elbow, and wrist of one arm into such a clique, while be the mountain binds the torso, hips, knees, and ankles. When we add these metaphor edges at strength β, the bound joints co-vary and the movement collapses onto a lower-dimensional manifold, a synergy in the sense of section 1, where a manifold means the reduced set of postures the body now actually visits. Running the model, adding pearls on a string at β = 3 drops the effective dimensionality below its baseline, exactly the compression Bernstein’s problem asks for.

Effective dimensionality, and why temperature cannot touch it

We need a single number for how many independent directions the movement actually uses, so that we can say whether a metaphor has compressed it. The participation ratio supplies that number. It reads off the covariance spectrum {λi}, the list of variances along the movement’s natural directions, where each λi measures how much the body moves along one such direction. The participation ratio combines these into an effective count of active directions.

PR(Σ) = (∑i λi)2∑i λi2  ∈  [1, n]

The formula gives a number between 1 and n. PR = n when the variance spreads equally over all directions, which means no coordination, every joint doing its own thing. PR approaches 1 when a single collective coordinate dominates, which means one synergy, the whole body moving along one combined pattern. Now the payoff of separating the two controls. Because Σ = T J−1 multiplies every variance by the same temperature T, the ratio above is unchanged, since the common factor cancels top and bottom. Raising the temperature enlarges the movement without reshaping it. The model’s own output states the point flatly, printing the identical participation ratio at T = 1 and T = 5. Dimensionality belongs to J alone, and variability belongs to T alone. This is why the model can hold apart the claim that a metaphor changes coordination from the claim that practice reduces variability, and study each on its own.

Signs: from binding to relation

A plain Laplacian can only make joints move together, giving a positive correlation, because it penalizes any difference between linked joints. Real coordination needs more than togetherness. Walking swings the two legs in opposition, and the model must be able to say that two joints move oppositely as easily as it says they move together. To let a metaphor specify the relation the joints hold, and not just that they coordinate, each coupling term penalizes a signed linear combination, a weighted difference whose coefficients carry the relation.

Eterm = ½ w (a xi − b xj)2

The term is cheapest when a xi equals b xj, so the coefficients a and b set which relation the two joints are pushed toward. Working out the cost, this term adds wa2 to Jii, wb2 to Jjj, and −wab to Jij. The sign of the product ab sets the relation. This borrows the central device of the Ising model from physics, in which each element carries a sign and neighbouring elements are coupled to agree or to disagree, and from which the language of signed couplings and frustration descends (Ising, 1925). Every term forms a perfect square, so it stays positive-semidefinite, meaning it never lowers the energy below zero, and J stays positive-definite no matter how many terms we add, so the energy bowl keeps its bottom however many couplings the model imposes.

Here the model reaches its second real commitment, and it separates two things the word coordination usually blurs, how much the movement is compressed and which relation the compression holds. Sign does not enter the participation ratio, because an anti-phase synergy collapses exactly as many dimensions as an in-phase one, tying two joints into one combined direction either way. Only the pattern of the surviving mode separates the two, in the structure of the correlation signs, positive where joints move together and negative where they oppose. A metaphor’s constraint has a magnitude, how far it collapses the movement, and the participation ratio measures that, answering Bernstein’s question of compression. The sign fixes the relation the coordination keeps instead, in-phase at a relative phase of zero and anti-phase at a relative phase of half a cycle. Relative phase is the timing offset between two rhythmic movements, zero when they reach their extremes at the same instant and half a cycle when one reaches its extreme as the other reaches the opposite. In the Haken-Kelso-Bunz equation, as Turvey reads it, that relation is “the relative phase φ between the two segments,” the collective variable that governs the coupling and admits “the co-existence of two such attractors (in-phase and antiphase)” (Turvey, 2007, 680). An attractor here is a stable pattern the movement tends to fall into and return to when disturbed. Our model reads the sign of a coupling as that collective variable, so on our reading a metaphor sets an order parameter for the coordination, choosing which stable relation the body settles into. Fixing the relative phase leaves the specific joint angles free to vary randomly under sampling, which is why two bodies that share no angles can share the same coordination.

Frustration: conflicting metaphors freeze the body

Two metaphors that specify incompatible relations on the same joints cannot both hold. This is the situation physicists call frustration, where one constraint pulls toward agreement and another toward opposition and no arrangement satisfies both. Suppose we load an in-phase coupling at weight W(1−λ) and an anti-phase coupling at weight Wλ on the same pair, with λ a dial that mixes the two from all-together at one extreme to all-opposed at the other. The off-diagonal becomes the signed sum of the two, and each diagonal gains from both.

Jij = −W(1−λ) + Wλ = −W(1−2λ)  → 0 at λ = ½, while Jii, Jjj each gain W

At λ = ½ the two demands are equal and the off-diagonal passes through zero, so the coupling that tied the joints together vanishes, even as both joints stiffen because each diagonal has absorbed weight from both terms. The collective coordinates c± = (xL ± xR)/√2, the combined together-mode and opposed-mode of the pair, stiffen too, and no low-energy coordinated motion remains. Conflicting instructions do not average into a compromise coordination. They cancel, and the coordination collapses through zero. In a Gaussian energy this loss removes a soft mode, an easy direction of motion, where a real spin glass, the frustrated magnet the physics comes from, would open a rugged landscape of many competing states, but the movement suffers the same way. The body loses its one low-energy coordinated direction and stiffens in place. Two incompatible teaching images therefore degrade coordination, since the two constraints cancel where a single image might have collapsed the movement onto one clean synergy. Section 6 turns this into a testable prediction about the order and compatibility of instruction.

4. Does a real body behave this way? A proof of concept

A model can be internally coherent and still describe no real body, so we tested its premises against recorded human movement. The formalism makes three assumptions about real movement. Movement uses few dimensions. Its conditional-dependence structure, the pattern of which joints are directly tied to which once the rest are accounted for, follows anatomy. And the sign of coordination changes from one movement to another even at a similar number of dimensions. We tested each assumption by fitting the Gaussian graphical model to CMU Graphics Lab motion-capture data, a public library of recordings in which reflective markers on a moving person are tracked in three dimensions. For each trial we took the per-frame joint Euler angles, the three numbers that specify each joint’s orientation in every recorded instant, removed global position and orientation so that walking across the room does not masquerade as coordination, centred and pooled the trials within a task, and estimated the covariance and precision across frames. The data cover three tasks (walk, run, jump), one subject and two trials each, and 50 rotational DOF once we drop near-constant joints, that is, joints that barely move and would only add noise.

(A) Movement is low-dimensional

We report two participation ratios, because they answer different questions. A few large-amplitude joints dominate the raw-covariance participation ratio, so it overstates the collapse, counting a movement as more compressed than it is simply because some joints swing wider than others. The standardized (correlation) participation ratio removes that size effect by putting every joint on the same scale, and so measures more honestly how many coordinated directions stay active. Even standardized, about five of the fifty dimensions account for the movement. Fifty possible directions, and roughly five in use, which is the compression Bernstein’s problem predicts a working body must find. This matches the synergy literature, where a few principal components, a few dominant patterns extracted from the data, account for most of the postural variance (Santello et al., 1998; Turvey, 2007).

(B) The precision graph recovers the kinematic tree

We build J from a graph Laplacian of the anatomical linkage, so the model assumes that the direct dependencies among joints run along the body’s physical connections. That assumption is checkable. If the construction is correct, the estimated partial correlations, the edge weights of the Gaussian graphical model, should peak between anatomically adjacent joints. A partial correlation measures how strongly two joints move together once every other joint is held fixed, so it isolates a direct tie from ties passed along through intermediaries. They do peak between neighbours, by a factor of about two. The separation between adjacent and non-adjacent pairs climbs well above chance, reported as an AUC, a single number for how cleanly a measure sorts two groups (AUC 0.5 = chance, 1 = perfect).

(C) Same body, different task, the sign flips

Here the central result appears, and it tests the claim the whole model rests on, that the sign of a coupling, not the amount of compression, carries the relation a coordination holds. Across the three tasks the correlation between the matched flexion axes of paired limbs, the bending of a left limb against the bending of its right partner, changes sign while the effective dimensionality barely moves. Walking and running couple the limbs anti-phase, since they alternate, one arm forward as the other swings back, and jumping couples them in-phase, since the limbs extend together to drive the body upward. The collapse stays the same in every case while the relation flips, which is exactly the dissociation the model predicts, magnitude fixed and sign free. Walk and run sit at a relative phase of half a cycle, jump at a relative phase of zero. Recovering that phase directly from the time series with a Hilbert transform confirms the reading, since the paired limbs lock near 180 degrees in walking and running and near 0 degrees in jumping, with phase-locking above 0.9, reported on the Data tab. Turvey describes just this transition in locomotion, where faster speeds “entail in-phase coordination of limbs of the same girdle in contrast to the anti-phase coordination that characterizes the dominant walking gait,” the organization coming “to switch spontaneously from antiphase to in-phase” (Turvey, 2007, 669). One body visits both modes at a near-constant participation ratio, which is the bistability, the coexistence of two stable patterns with switching between them, that the Haken-Kelso-Bunz model predicts, now measured in whole-body locomotion rather than in a pair of fingers (Haken, Kelso & Bunz, 1985).

The premises prove true. All three assumptions the model needs about real movement hold in the data, so the approach earns a second version rather than abandonment. We still cannot claim that a metaphor makes two people’s coordination converge, because a task only substitutes for an instruction here, and a single body supplies every measurement. Different tasks stand in for different instructions, and one person stands in for teacher and learner alike. A test of convergence needs a second body and a spoken image, which we set out in section 6.

(D) The same premises on real tai-chi, and a first read on skill

Walk, run, and jump test the machinery but are not the movement the project studies, and a single body supplies every measurement above. So we refit the same model to real expert Taijiquan, the UMONS-TAICHI recording of twelve people performing thirteen techniques and scored for skill by three judges, and we did it on two instruments running together, a markerless Kinect and a marker-based Qualisys optical system, so that a result can be checked against the way the body was measured. The first two premises hold on this movement as well. Within a gesture the joint angles collapse onto a handful of effective dimensions on both instruments, and on the clean marker data the coupling graph recovers the body’s anatomical tree at the same strength the CMU fit reached. The Data tab reports both in full.

The third premise, the one the model rests on, gets its first test against skill. The model does not read skill as a larger static amount of coupling. It reads skill as a steadier order parameter, a relative phase the body holds to more firmly cycle after cycle. That prediction needs timing, which the thirty-frame Kinect cannot resolve and the hundred-and-seventy-nine-frame Qualisys can, so it runs on the markers. Measured there, the steadiness with which the two arms hold a fixed relative phase rises with judged skill, while a static measure of how much the joints couple does not track skill at all, and a first Kinect result that seemed to show a static coupling rise reversed on the better instrument. Expertise shows up as the stability of the order parameter, the very quantity section 3 built the model around. This is the strongest support the model has yet earned and the sharpest prediction it hands the experiment in section 6, and the two-instrument check that produced it, together with the artifact it caught, is laid out on the Data tab.

5. Honest limits

We list the model’s shortcomings plainly, since a proof of concept earns trust by naming what it has not yet shown.

  • The task substitutes for the instruction. Walk, run, and jump differ in coordination, but we gave no metaphor and measured no learner following one. To test the real claim, that a verbal image reshapes J, we need a study that varies the instruction while holding the task fixed, so that any change in coordination can be credited to the words rather than to the activity.
  • Sign conventions depend on the axis. Which direction of a joint’s bending we label positive is a bookkeeping choice, so only the relative sign structure is meaningful, for example the contrast between contralateral and ipsilateral pairs, or between walk and jump. The absolute sign of any single entry has no meaning on its own.
  • The representations do not match exactly. Our model uses one scalar angle per joint, a single number for how far it bends. Motion capture records three rotational DOF per joint in a real articulated chain, since a real joint can rotate about three axes at once. To fit the model we need either a projection of each joint onto its principal rotation axis, keeping the one direction it mostly uses, or a lift of the model to three DOF per joint, a real methods decision that shapes the fit.
  • Temperature models expertise only thinly. T scales all directions equally, but an expert’s variance reduction is anisotropic, meaning it tightens some directions far more than others, holding steady the combinations that matter for the task while leaving the rest loose. This is the uncontrolled-manifold finding of Jörn Scholz and Gregor Schöner, who showed that skilled performers concentrate their variability into directions that do not disturb the goal (Scholz & Schöner, 1999). An anisotropic reduction itself changes J, not just T. So in this model “the learner consolidated the metaphor” and “the learner became expert” can describe one event, and the accounts of motor learning that separate fast and slow adaptive processes suggest the two may in fact run on different timescales (Smith, Ghazizadeh & Shadmehr, 2006). The tai-chi fit of section 4(D) bears this out and sharpens what the second version needs. Skill there does not show up as a lower dimension count or as a smaller overall variability, the two things T and a global collapse would predict, but as a steadier hold on the relative phase, the order parameter. A temperature that scales every direction alike cannot represent that, so the second version needs a term for the coupling strength of the coordinated mode against its own noise, the depth of the attractor rather than the width of the whole bowl.
  • The samples are small. With few frames in the run condition, its fit serves as a proof of concept, good for direction but short of an estimate with error bars, so we read the numbers as evidence of a pattern rather than as precise measurements.

6. Intended outcome, from working model to fundable experiment

Up to this point we have a coherent mechanism, consistent with real movement. We have not yet shown that a spoken metaphor makes a learner’s coordination converge on a teacher’s across two different bodies, our actual thesis, and only a laboratory study can show it. The remaining steps fall into two kinds. The computational steps cost only time and run now on the model itself. One of them turns the model into an experiment worth funding.

  1. Two-body convergence (computational, runs now). We give a teacher and a learner different bodies, that is, a different baseline J and different segment lengths, so that no single posture means the same thing for both. A joint-angle instruction stays specific to one body and fails across the mismatch, since the learner copying the teacher’s exact angles would produce a different coordination in a differently proportioned frame. A metaphor sets a relative phase instead, which each body reaches in its own coordinates. If the metaphor drives both bodies onto the same relative-phase mode where a list of joint angles fails, the model stops illustrating that a metaphor could align two people and lets us show that it does. This step needs no new data, and we have now run it. We trained a teacher to an anti-phase arm coordination and started two learners of different anatomy from an in-phase coordination, and under a shared metaphor both learners converged on the teacher’s relative phase, the gap closing from 1.3 to 0.1, while a joint-angle instruction left the gap flat at 1.27. The two learners converge on the teacher’s relative phase where a joint-angle channel does not, a result the Data tab reports in full. The pitch below rests on it.
  2. A metaphor-instruction experiment. ◆ DISI PITCH We ask DISI to fund this experiment. We would record full-body kinematics under different metaphorical instructions, with expert and novice or teacher and learner pairs, in a pedagogy that already teaches through metaphor, starting with tai chi and extending to dance, physical therapy, and instrumental posture. We can pre-register the whole study in advance, meaning we commit to our predictions publicly before collecting data, including the predicted direction of the participation-ratio collapse, the correlation-sign pattern, the rise of the order parameter’s stability with skill, and the convergence of teacher and learner onto a shared relative phase, together with the analysis. A funded study would then test that prediction rather than search for one. Three conditions already hold. The first condition is that preliminary results exist, on this page, in step 1, and in the fit to real expert tai-chi of section 4(D), where the order parameter’s stability already tracks judged skill. The second condition is that precedent exists, since analogy produces implicit motor learning, the kind a learner picks up without being able to state the rule (Liao & Masters, 2001), and since instruction and the focus of attention reshape motor performance (Wulf & Lewthwaite, 2016). The third condition is that the method works, since we have now fit the model to Tits et al.’s (2018) expert tai-chi recording and found the collapse, the anatomical graph, and a skill-tracking order parameter in it. The topic fits DISI directly, since embodied skill passing between agents without symbols is itself a kind of intelligence, and this fit requires no distortion of the project’s own framing.
  3. Frustration as curriculum. We test the prediction from section 3 that two conflicting teaching images degrade coordination, the two constraints cancelling where a single image would have collapsed the movement onto one synergy. We have now run the computational version, and at equal conflict the shared coordination freezes as predicted, its cheapest mode losing about sevenfold in variance while the correlation passes through zero, a result the Data tab reports in full. The prediction targets the order and compatibility of instruction, the question of which images a teacher can combine and in what sequence, squarely a question about how one body settles into a coordination, and it makes a natural second study once we fund the first.
  4. Scale the premises across many bodies. We have now refit the dimensionality and anatomy premises on real tai-chi across a dozen bodies (Tits et al., 2018; Data tab, section 2). The next step scales them further on a large corpus such as AMASS or the KIT Whole-Body Human Motion Database, using different activities as a first rough proxy for different relational content, so that the §4 findings rest on hundreds of bodies rather than a dozen.
  5. Make the model and the data use the same representation. We choose either the scalar-axis projection or the three-DOF lift from §5, so that the model and the data use the same geometry and the fit follows a principled method rather than a convenience.

Provenance. We build the model as a Gaussian graphical model over the body’s degrees of freedom, with a graph-Laplacian precision matrix, a signed-coupling extension (in-phase, anti-phase, and fixed-ratio) that we take from the Ising and spin-glass literature, and a temperature that scales variability without changing coordination. The model’s target is Bernstein’s degrees-of-freedom problem. We read the sign of a coupling as a relative-phase order parameter after Haken, Kelso and Bunz, so a metaphor sets a coordination regime rather than transferring the relational structure Gentner’s structure-mapping would posit. As a proof of concept, we fit the model to CMU Graphics Lab Motion Capture (subjects walking, running, and jumping), using the participation ratio for effective dimensionality, partial correlations for the conditional-dependence graph, and matched-axis correlations for the sign of limb coordination. We then refit it to real expert Taijiquan (UMONS-TAICHI, Tits et al. 2018) on two instruments at once, a markerless Kinect and a marker-based Qualisys optical system, where the collapse and the anatomical graph hold and the order parameter’s stability tracks judged skill, as section 4(D) and the Data tab report. The repository holds the code and data fetch under model/. The bridge dossier sets out the theoretical frame, analogy as structure-mapping meeting embodied skill, and What is DATM describes the corpus method.
