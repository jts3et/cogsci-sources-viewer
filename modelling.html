Methodology — the coordination model

The modelling strand of the project, from inception to intended outcome: what the model is, where it comes from, why it is built the way it is, whether its premises hold against real movement, and the experiment it is built to make possible.

1. Inception — why a model, and why this one

The project, shared across five collaborators, asks how tacit, embodied skill passes from a teacher to a learner when the knowledge cannot be told — Polanyi's problem, set in movement (Polanyi, 1966; Miton & DeDeo, 2022). The working answer is that a metaphor carries it: a teaching image such as pearls on a string or cloud hands projects a relational structure onto the learner's body, which is structure-mapping (Gentner, 1983) run where the aligned structure is felt rather than judged. The corpus side of the project — the topic map and the bridge dossier — shows this seam is real in the literature. It cannot exhibit the mechanism in a body, or say what a metaphor constrains movement concretely means.

The commitment is to metaphor, not analogy. What a tai-chi teacher utters is a metaphor, mostly an A is B image rather than a four-term proportion, so the word that names the data is metaphor. Structure-mapping stays on as the account of the relational content the metaphor carries, run in a register the embodied-metaphor tradition treats as sensorimotor rather than amodal (Gentner, 1988; Abrahamson, 2020; Kimmel et al., 2024). Metaphor is the noun, structure-mapping the operation it performs.

The modelling strand began as a first attempt at that mechanism — a rough model that writes a metaphor as a set of couplings on the body's degrees of freedom, drawing on three traditions: Gaussian graphical models and the Ising line from statistical physics (the couplings, their signs, and frustration between conflicting constraints), Bernstein's degrees-of-freedom problem from motor control (the joint excess a skill has to tame), and Gentner's structure-mapping (the relational content that must survive the mapping). As a first pass it was partial, and the open question was whether its approach was worth keeping or should be set aside.

Introducing it here answers that empirically rather than by argument. State its formalism cleanly enough to be judged, fit it to real motion-capture, and mark exactly where it still fails. The finding is that the first attempt is wrong in its execution and right in its bones: its three premises — movement is low-dimensional, coordination tracks anatomy, and relational content lives in the sign of coupling — each hold against real movement (§4). That is the case for a serious second version rather than abandoning the approach, and the limits (§5) and intended outcome (§6) are its specification.

The one decision worth keeping intact from the first attempt is the one the rest of the page turns on: two knobs that do not interfere. Coupling structure sets how many dimensions of movement survive; temperature sets only how variable the movement is. Holding these apart keeps two claims separate that the literature routinely runs together — that a metaphor constrains coordination, and that practice reduces variability.

2. What the model claims, before the algebra

A body has many joints, hence many degrees of freedom (DOF). The model represents a body as a probability distribution over joint configurations and gives the modeller the two knobs named above.

  • Coupling structure (the matrix J) controls the effective dimensionality of movement — how many independent directions the body actually explores. A metaphor adds couplings, binding otherwise-independent joints into a synergy and collapsing the dimension.
  • Temperature (the scalar T) controls variability only — the overall spread of movement, with the shape of coordination left untouched.

Because these two knobs are mathematically orthogonal, the model keeps two easily-confused claims apart. A metaphor constrains coordination is a statement about J. Practice reduces variability is a statement about T. The next section makes that separation exact, adds signs so couplings carry relational content and not merely bind, and then the section after asks whether real bodies behave the way the model needs.

3. The formalism

State and energy

Write x ∈ ℝn for the vector of joint-angle deviations from a fixed neutral pose, one scalar per DOF. A configuration's cost is a quadratic energy set by a symmetric positive-definite precision matrix J.

E(x) = ½ x⊤J x

Distribution

Configurations are Boltzmann-distributed in that energy at temperature T. The result is a zero-mean Gaussian whose covariance is the inverse precision, scaled by T.

p(x) ∝ exp(−E(x)/T)  ⇒  x ~ 𝒩(0, Σ),  Σ = T J−1

The whole content of the movement lives in the covariance Σ — in how joints co-vary, not in any mean pose, which is fixed at neutral. That is the model's first substantive commitment, and it is the reason a metaphor rather than a list of joint angles is the right kind of instruction. A joint-angle target names a point; a metaphor names a shape of covariation.

Coupling structure from a graph Laplacian

The precision matrix is built as a baseline stiffness plus a weighted graph Laplacian of the coupling graph. For an edge set with weights, the Laplacian is L = D − W (degree minus adjacency); adding γI gives every DOF a finite variance on its own and keeps J positive-definite.

J = γI + Lanatomy + β Lmetaphor  L PSD, γ > 0 ⇒ J ≻ 0

Here Lanatomy is the body's own linkage graph (a weak, fixed scaffold), β is the strength of the imposed metaphor, and Lmetaphor is a clique over the joints the metaphor binds. Adding metaphor edges makes the bound joints co-vary and collapses movement onto a lower-dimensional manifold — a synergy.

Effective dimensionality, and why temperature cannot touch it

Effective DOF is read off the covariance spectrum {λi} by the participation ratio.

PR(Σ) = (∑i λi)2∑i λi2  ∈  [1, n]

PR = n when every direction carries equal variance (no coordination); PR → 1 when one collective coordinate dominates (a single synergy). Because Σ = T J−1 multiplies every eigenvalue by the same T, the ratio above is unchanged: raising the temperature inflates the movement without reshaping it. This is the exact separation the model was built for — dimensionality is a property of J alone, variability a property of T alone.

Signs: from binding to relation

A plain Laplacian can only make joints move together (positive correlation). To let a metaphor specify not just that joints coordinate but the relation they hold, each coupling term is written as a penalty on a signed linear combination.

Eterm = ½ w (a xi − b xj)2

Its contribution to the precision matrix is ΔJii = wa2, ΔJjj = wb2, ΔJij = −wab. The sign of the product ab sets the relation, and because every term is a perfect square it is positive-semidefinite, so J stays positive-definite no matter how many are added.

This yields the model's second substantive commitment. The participation ratio is sign-blind: an anti-phase synergy collapses exactly as many dimensions as an in-phase one. What tells them apart is the pattern of the surviving collective mode — the correlation-sign structure. So a metaphor's magnitude of constraint (how much collapse, answering Bernstein) is carried by PR, and its relational content (which coordination, where the analogy's structure lives) is carried by the signs. That is structure-mapping in a motor register: attributes (specific angles) wash out under sampling, and only relations (the couplings) survive.

Frustration: conflicting metaphors freeze the body

Two metaphors that demand incompatible relations on the same joints cannot be jointly satisfied. Load an in-phase coupling at weight W(1−λ) and an anti-phase coupling at weight Wλ on the same pair. The off-diagonal is the signed sum, and the diagonal gains from both.

Jij = −W(1−λ) + Wλ = −W(1−2λ)  → 0 at λ = ½, while Jii, Jjj each gain W

At λ = ½ the coupling passes through zero while both joints stiffen, so the collective coordinates c± = (xL ± xR)/√2 are both suppressed and no cheap coordinated motion remains. Conflicting instructions do not average — they cancel, and coordination collapses through zero. In a Gaussian energy this is the loss of a soft mode rather than the rugged degeneracy of a true spin glass, but the movement consequence is the intended one: incompatible teaching images should degrade coordination, not blend into a compromise.

4. Does a real body behave this way? A proof of concept

The formalism rests on three premises about real movement: that it is genuinely low-dimensional, that its conditional-dependence structure follows anatomy, and that the sign of coordination is what changes across different movements at similar dimensionality. Each was tested by fitting the Gaussian graphical model directly to CMU Graphics Lab motion-capture data — per-frame joint Euler angles, global position and orientation removed, trials centred and pooled within a task, covariance and precision estimated across frames. Three tasks, one subject each, two trials apiece (walk, run, jump); 50 rotational DOF after dropping near-constant joints.

(A) Movement is low-dimensional

Two participation ratios are reported because they answer different questions. The raw-covariance PR is dominated by a few large-amplitude joints and overstates the collapse; the standardized (correlation) PR is the honest measure of how many coordinated directions are active. Even standardized, roughly five dimensions out of fifty carry the movement, consistent with the synergy literature in which a few principal components carry most of the postural variance (Santello et al., 1998; Turvey, 2007).

The model builds J from a graph Laplacian of the anatomical linkage. If that construction is right, the estimated partial correlations — the Gaussian graphical model's edge weights — should concentrate on anatomically adjacent joints. They do, by a factor of roughly two, and the separation between adjacent and non-adjacent pairs is well above chance (AUC 0.5 = chance, 1 = perfect).

(C) Same body, different task — the sign flips

This is the load-bearing result. The correlation of the matched flexion axis of paired limbs reorganizes across tasks while the effective dimensionality barely moves. Walking and running couple the limbs anti-phase (they alternate); jumping couples them in-phase (they extend together). Same collapse, opposite relational content — the sign-blind-PR claim, in real human movement.

The model earns its premises. What it does not yet earn is the claim that a metaphor transmits a skill, because a task is only a proxy for an instruction, and one body is not a teacher and a learner. That is what the intended outcome, below, is built to reach.

5. Honest limits

  • Task is a proxy for instruction. Walk / run / jump differ in coordination, but no metaphor was given. The distinctive claim — that a verbal image reshapes J — needs a study that manipulates the instruction while holding the task fixed.
  • Sign conventions are axis-relative. Only the relative sign structure (contralateral opposite to ipsilateral, walk opposite to jump) is interpretable, not the absolute sign of any single entry.
  • Representation mismatch. The model uses one scalar angle per joint; motion capture supplies three-DOF joint rotations in a true articulated chain. A fit requires either projecting each joint onto its principal rotation axis or lifting the model to three-DOF joints — a real methods decision, not a detail.
  • Temperature is a thin model of expertise. T scales all directions equally, but expert variance reduction is anisotropic (the uncontrolled-manifold finding; Scholz & Schöner, 1999). Anisotropic reduction is a reshaping of J, which means "the learner consolidated the metaphor" and "the learner became expert" may be the same event in this model — a feature to claim deliberately or a conflation to resolve.
  • Small samples. The run condition has few frames; the fit is a proof of concept, not an estimate with error bars.

6. Intended outcome — from a working model to a fundable experiment

Everything to this point establishes that the mechanism is coherent and consistent with real movement. What remains is to show that a metaphor carries a skill from one body to another — the project's actual thesis — and to take that claim into a lab. The steps split cleanly: the computational ones cost nothing but time and can run now, and one of them is where this stops being a model and becomes an experiment worth funding.

  1. Two-body transmission — computational, run it now. Give a teacher and a learner different bodies (different baseline J, different segment lengths). A joint-angle instruction is body-specific and will not survive the mismatch; a relational coupling (the sign-and-ratio pattern) is body-invariant and should. If the metaphor reconstructs the teacher's synergy where a DOF list fails, the model stops illustrating that metaphor could transmit tacit skill and starts arguing that it does. This needs no new data, and it is the preliminary result the pitch below rests on.
  2. The metaphor-instruction experiment. ◆ DISI PITCH This is where we ask DISI for a real experiment. Record full-body kinematics under varied metaphorical instruction, with expert↔novice or teacher↔learner pairs, in a metaphor-native pedagogy — tai chi first, then dance, physical therapy, instrumental posture. The model arrives pre-registered: it predicts the direction of the PR collapse, the correlation-sign pattern, and the cross-body transfer, and it hands over the analysis pipeline in advance, so the ask is to run a specified test, not to go exploring. Three things make it fundable now — the preliminary results already exist (this page, plus step 1); the effect has precedent (analogy drives implicit motor learning, Liao & Masters, 2001; instruction and attention reshape motor performance, Wulf & Lewthwaite, 2016); and the data collection is demonstrably feasible, since Tits et al. (2018) already captured expert Taijiquan motion at exactly this grain. The relevance-to-funder line writes itself for DISI — embodied skill as a non-symbolic intelligence that moves between agents by metaphor — without touching the project's own framing.
  3. Frustration as curriculum. Test the prediction that two conflicting teaching images degrade coordination rather than blend. This is a claim about the ordering and compatibility of instruction, squarely a transmission-of-learning result, and a natural second study once the first is funded.
  4. Scale the premises up. Refit the dimensionality and anatomy results on a large corpus — AMASS or the KIT Whole-Body Human Motion Database — using distinct activities as a first, weak proxy for distinct relational content. A tai-chi–specific set already sits in the reading list (Tits et al., 2018, expert Taijiquan mocap), so feasibility is not hypothetical.
  5. Bridge the representation. Commit to either the scalar-axis projection or the three-DOF lift, so the model and the data speak the same geometry and the fit is principled rather than illustrative.
